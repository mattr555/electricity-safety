Electrical Safety
Congratulations on the purchase your new home!
Now is a good time to review the safety rules when using electrical appliances.
But first, some physics is in order.
Parallel Circuits
The circuits in your home are considered to be "parallel" circuits.
There are multiple circuits for different parts of your home; like one for the water heater, one for the kitchen, and one for the bathroom, for example.
This means that the same amount of power is delivered to each item.
This amount of power is measured in volts. In the United States, this value is 120 V.

However, this also means that the current of the electricity is broken up.
This explains why the lights dim when you turn a vacuum or a hair dryer on. These high-powered items draw more current that is taken away from the other circuits in your house.
Your circuit box
Your circuit box may look like this. It has a main switch that controls all the power going into the house.
It also has a circuit breaker for each circuit in your home. This allows you to switch off only part of the power going into your house when working with electrical components.
It is a good idea to figure out which breakers control which components to eliminate guessing when in use

Safety tip: You should always switch off the power to a circuit when you're working with potentially live wires, such as with a stove or oven replacement.
Safety Features
Your home probably already has some features built in to the circuit box.
These help to reduce the chance of an electrical fire if too much power is used.
Circuit Breakers
When too much current is drawn from the circuit, wires may heat up through a process called Joule heating and begin to melt or burn, causing an electrical fire.
To combat this, circuit breakers are used. Electricity flows through an electromagnet (a magnet that uses electricity to work).
Enough current will be able to pull the switch down, disconnecting the circuit from the main power and preventing a fire.
These are advantageous because they are easy to switch and don't have to be replaced every time too much current is drawn.
Fuses
Older circuit boxes may have fuses, which work like incandescent light bulbs.
Here, thin wire filaments heat up like in a light bulb. If too much current is drawn, the wire will get too hot and disintegrate.
If a fuse is "blown," it will need to be replaced.
Fuses are also found in cars.
Grounding Wires
Electricity wants to travel to where it is more spread out.
This is why lightning wants to move from a crowded cloud to the ground.
In the same way, electricity wants to move from it's cramped wires and components to a place more spread out. When you are working in a circuit box, that place may be you.
To make sure that a worker doesn't experience an electric shock by touching the wrong things, the circuit box is "grounded" to the earth with a wire. This creates a pathway to a more spread out location when work is done on a circuit.

Grounding wires are also used with lightning rods to disperse the energy if it is hit. The electricity will dissipate into the ground, rather than into the house.
First Aid
  1. Make sure the area is safe for entry
  2. Do NOT touch a victim who is in immediate contact with the source of electricity.
  3. Try to turn off the main source of energy with the circuit box. If this is not doable, use a non-conductive material, like wood, to separate the victim from the source of electricity
  4. Call 9-1-1
  5. Begin to perform CPR if the breathing or heartbeat appears to be affected by the electricity.
  6. Treat the victim for shock by elevating his feet above his head.
